\fragmenthead

\noindent
This fragment is a deliberate stress test. Pullum's handout shows how a
SLASH-free, logic-on-trees treatment can describe ordinary unbounded
dependencies, then flags Across-the-Board extraction as the hard residue where
Gazdar's GPSG analysis is especially elegant.

\section*{1.~Phenomenon}

Across-the-Board extraction allows one preposed phrase to correspond to gaps
inside each coordinate:

\ea
\textit{Those, I either sent back or paid for.}
\z

\ea
\ungram{\textit{Those, I either paid the bill or sent back.}}
\z

\ea
\ungram{\textit{Those, I either sent back or paid the bill.}}
\z

The good case has matching gaps in both coordinates. The bad cases have only
one gap: one coordinate is independently compatible with the extracted NP, but
the other is not. The fragment asks whether this can be stated directly over
trees, without reintroducing a SLASH-valued category.

\section*{2.~Vocabulary}

Reuse Pullum's vacancy and bindability machinery, but add coordination
relations:

\ea
$\Coord(c)$: $c$ is a coordinate construction.\\
$\Conjunct(c,k)$: $k$ is a conjunct daughter of coordinate construction $c$.\\
$\ParallelGap(z_1,z_2)$: vacancies $z_1$ and $z_2$ are syntactically parallel.
\z

The last predicate is a deliberate pressure point. For NP extraction, it might
mean that both vacancies are complements of comparable lexical heads, or that
both are linked to the same extracted prenucleus NP by the same syntactic
relation. It is not a semantic or thematic role predicate. If that extra
condition cannot be reduced to syntactic relations such as function-labelled
dominance plus a shared prenucleus relation, the fragment has located a real
bridge problem rather than solved it.

\section*{3.~Specification}

Use the same basic vacancy definitions:

\ea
$\Vacancy(x) \defas \Phrasal(x) \wedge \neg(\exists y)[x \imdom y]$
\z

\ea
$\NPVac(x) \defas \Vacancy(x) \wedge \NP(x)$
\z

Assume the Pullum-style definition of $\NPBindable(r)$: $r$ roots a
bridge-linked dominance chain containing an NP vacancy. Then define a conjunct
as NP-binding if it has such a root:

\ea
$\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{ConjNPBindable}(k) \defas {} &
  (\exists r)[k \dm r \wedge \NPBindable(r)].
\end{aligned}$\\
\gloss{conjunct $k$ contains an NP-bindable domain}
\z

The weak ATB condition requires every conjunct to contain a bindable NP gap:

\ea
$\begin{aligned}
\ATB(c) \defas {} & \Coord(c) \wedge
  (\forall k)[\Conjunct(c,k) \Rightarrow \mathrm{ConjNPBindable}(k)].
\end{aligned}$\\
\gloss{all conjuncts contain an NP-bindable domain}
\z

That is not quite enough. It rules out the one-gap cases, but it does not yet
guarantee that the gaps are parallel in the right way. A stronger condition
quantifies over the actual vacancies:

\ea
$\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{ATB}^{+}(c) \defas {} & \Coord(c) \wedge
  (\forall k)[\Conjunct(c,k) \Rightarrow
    (\exists z)[k \dm z \wedge \NPVac(z)]] \\
& {} \wedge
  (\forall k_1)(\forall k_2)(\forall z_1)(\forall z_2) \\
& \quad [\Conjunct(c,k_1) \wedge \Conjunct(c,k_2) \wedge
    k_1 \dm z_1 \wedge k_2 \dm z_2 \wedge {} \\
& \qquad \NPVac(z_1) \wedge \NPVac(z_2)
    \Rightarrow \ParallelGap(z_1,z_2)].
\end{aligned}$\\
\gloss{all conjuncts contain NP vacancies, and those vacancies are syntactically parallel}
\z

\section*{4.~Worked instance}

In \mention{Those, I either sent back or paid for}, the coordinate VP contains
two conjuncts. The first has an NP vacancy as complement of the particle-verb
complex \mention{sent back}; the second has an NP vacancy as complement of
\mention{for}. Each conjunct therefore contains an NP-bindable domain, and
the coordinate satisfies the weak $\ATB$ condition. If the two missing NPs are
both linked to the same prenucleus NP \mention{those}, they also satisfy the
intended $\ParallelGap$ condition. This is a syntactic bridge condition, not a
claim that the two gaps have the same semantic role.

In \mention{Those, I either paid the bill or sent back}, the first conjunct
has no NP vacancy corresponding to \mention{those}. It therefore fails
$\ATB(c)$. The mirror-image example fails for the same reason in the second
conjunct.

\section*{5.~Open questions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),leftmargin=2em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item $\ParallelGap$ is doing real work. If it cannot be reduced to ordinary
        function-labelled dominance plus the shared prenucleus relation, the
        fragment has merely hidden a SLASH-like dependency under a new name.
  \item Semantic or thematic role matching is deliberately absent. If ATB
        requires such a condition, it needs a separate interface predicate with
        its own warrant.
  \item The formula needs a sharper account of coordination structure:
        binary vs.\ flat coordination, markers such as \mention{either} and
        \mention{or}, and possible non-constituent coordination.
  \item The weak condition may overgenerate cases with accidental gaps that
        are not jointly licensed by the same filler.
  \item This should be compared directly with Gazdar's 1981 treatment before
        being treated as a successful replacement.
\end{enumerate}
